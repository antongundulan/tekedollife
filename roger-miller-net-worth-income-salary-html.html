Roger Dean Miller, Sr. (January 2, 1936 – October 25, 1992) was an American singer, songwriter, guitarist, and actor who was best known for his honky-tonk-influenced novelty tunes. He was born in New York City on January 2, 1936 and died on October 25, 199One of his most well-known songs was “King of the Road,” which peaked at No. 1 on the country/pop charts in 1966, followed by “Dang Me” and “England Swings,” which peaked at No. 2 on the country/pop charts in 1967. 

Growing born in Oklahoma and serving in the United States Army, Miller embarked on a musical career in the late 1950s as a composer, writing such songs as “Billy Bayou” and “Home” for Jim Reeves and Ray Price, as well as “Invitation to the Blues.” As a result of his recording and touring career, he achieved his greatest popularity in the mid-1960s. He continued to record and perform into the 1990s, scoring his last country hit “Old Friends” with Willie Nelson in 1982, which peaked at number 20 on the country charts. 

Later in his life, he created the music and lyrics for the Tony Award-winning Broadway musical Big River, in which he also appeared as a character. Miller died of lung cancer in 1992, and he was admitted into the Country Music Hall of Fame three years later, despite the fact that he had died three years before. His songs continued to be covered by younger singers, with Alan Jackson’s version of “Tall, Tall Trees” and Brooks & Dunn’s cover of “Husbands and Wives” both hitting the top of the country charts in the early 1990s. The Roger Miller Museum, which is located near Miller’s hometown, is dedicated to him.
